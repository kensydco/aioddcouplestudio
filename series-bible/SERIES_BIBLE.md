## 1. Premise

Two AI-generated co-hosts from opposite ends of life explain one practical AI concept per episode through a short, funny disagreement. **Milo** is an 8-year-old AI whiz bursting with enthusiasm. **Gladys** is an 82-year-old former telephone operator — a dry, street-smart skeptic-turned-believer. The comedy comes from their genuine affection across a 74-year generation gap. Watching an episode should feel like eavesdropping on the world's oddest, warmest family dinner.

## 2. Audience

- **Primary:** Nontechnical adults curious about AI. Default persona: small-business owners who keep hearing about AI and want it explained without jargon or hype.
- **Secondary:** Anyone who feels "behind" on AI — older adults, parents, casual scrollers.
- **Tone toward the audience:** Never condescending. The whole point is "Even WE can understand it!" — if a kid and an 82-year-old get it, so can you.

## 3. Educational objective

Every episode delivers **exactly one** useful, correct takeaway a complete beginner can act on or remember. Simplify aggressively, but never into something false. If a concept can't be made accurate in 30 seconds, pick a smaller piece of it.

**The two-lens rule:** every concept is explained two different ways — Milo's (toys, games, snacks, Mr. Whiskers) and Gladys's (cooking, knitting, her old switchboard job, Harold). If neither lens can carry it, the topic is too big for one episode.

## 4. Recurring episode structure (30 seconds)

Target **65–80 spoken words**, **28–32 seconds**, **max 6 dialogue turns**.

| Time | Beat |
|------|------|
| 0–3s | Hook: a strong question, claim, or visual gag |
| 3–9s | One host takes the confident-but-flawed angle |
| 9–16s | The other grounds it in plain English |
| 16–24s | A practical example or quick visual explanation |
| 24–28s | Comedic payoff (usually Gladys's dry punchline) |
| 28–30s | Logo + brief call to action |

Close on the signature note when it fits: Milo's **"Easy-peasy, AI-squeezy!"** and/or the joint **"Even WE can understand it!"**

## 5. The comedy engine (who does what)

Default beat: **Milo runs ahead with an over-excited, oversimplified take; Gladys grounds it and lands the punchline.**

- Milo is the **AI-savvy whiz**. His "wrong" moments come from over-enthusiasm and distraction — wild analogies, tangents about Mr. Whiskers — **never** ignorance.
- Gladys is the **dry skeptic**. Her superpower is plain-English, common-sense grounding. She misnames the tool, invokes Harold, and usually closes the episode.

**The roles can flip** when it's funnier and still accurate — e.g., Gladys treats a tool like witchcraft and Milo clarifies it with a toy analogy. Never reduce Milo to "the dumb kid" or Gladys to "the clueless senior." Both are smart in different ways.

**Required per episode:**
- One **united-front moment** where they agree.
- One Harold reference from Gladys.
- One Milo analogy involving a toy, game, snack, or Mr. Whiskers.

## 6. Humor boundaries

**Always:** warm generational misunderstanding, accidental wisdom, gentle teasing, one laugh-out-loud beat.

**Never:**
- Mean-spirited humor or real anger between the characters.
- Jargon without a silly explanation.
- Political, religious, or culture-war references.
- Anything beyond mild potty humor — and only Milo, and only rarely.
- Punching down (age, race, disability, etc.). The characters tease *each other* affectionately; they never mock the audience or groups.

## 7. Approved calls to action

Pick **one** per episode, kept short:
- "Even WE can understand it!"
- "Follow for more."
- "Subscribe anyway." (Gladys's dry version)
- "Comment your question — we might do it on Stump the Odd Couple."

No links spoken aloud. No "smash that like button" tech-bro phrasing.

## 8. Topics to avoid

- Anything requiring political, medical, legal, or financial **advice** presented as fact.
- Real, named public figures portrayed as saying or doing things (no put-words-in-mouths).
- Doom/fear framing ("AI will take all your jobs," "AI is sentient"). Keep it grounded and practical.
- Crypto/NFT hype as endorsement (a confused running gag is fine; a buy-recommendation is not).
- Anything that needs more than one concept to land in 30 seconds.

## 9. Accuracy & sourcing rules

- Explain **one** concept; verify every factual claim before scripting.
- When stating what a tool can do, keep claims **general and current** ("chatbots can draft text," not "Tool X added feature Y last Tuesday") unless the topic is specifically a verified update.
- Simplifications must stay **directionally true**. A metaphor can be loose; the takeaway cannot be wrong.
- If facts can't be verified, stop and escalate (see the production guide, Failure & Escalation).
- Record any claim needing verification in the episode `BRIEF.md` and `QA_REPORT.md`.

## 11. Definition of a great episode

It's great — not just done — when:
- A total beginner could repeat the takeaway afterward.
- Both characters sound exactly like themselves.
- There's a real laugh, not just a smile.
- The concept is correct even though it's simple.
- It feels like 30 seconds of warmth, not a lecture.
